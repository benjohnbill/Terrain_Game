# Mobilization Historical Dynamics — Research

Status: RESEARCH (evidence layer — input to seals, never normative on its own).
Date: 2026-07-07. Feature: match-arc (conscription register sizing).

## Purpose and game context

The match is a compressed "deciding-war window": a Warring-States /
Three-Kingdoms-style hegemonic contest resolved in 15–25 turns. A realm's land
sustains a standing-force ceiling (600 men per abstract population point). This
research sizes the TOTAL lifetime draftable bodies for the window — the
conscription register, permanent; the dead never return in-match. Historical
anchors sought: (a) what fraction of a population is realistically mobilizable
across a decisive-war window, (b) the ratio of window-total-mobilized to peak
standing force (how many times a state "refills" its peak army), and (c) how
economy determined military quotas (the kokudaka case).

Source discipline: encyclopedic pages (Wikipedia, EBSCO, Britannica) are used
only where they report figures attributed to named scholarship or primary
administrative records; contested ancient figures are given as scholarly ranges
and flagged.

## 1. Warring States China and the Qin unification (c. 350–221 BC)

**Population.** Ge Jianxiong's demographic reconstruction puts Warring
States-era China at roughly **30–45 million** (reported in the survey of the
army-size debate on [Warring States period —
Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Warring_States_period)). The Qin
empire at unification is conventionally put around **~20 million**
([Qin dynasty — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qin_dynasty)). No
per-state census survives; individual great powers (Qin, Chu, Zhao) are
usually reconstructed at a few million each. All of these are model-based
estimates, not census data.

**Claimed army sizes.** The *Shiji* reports field armies of hundreds of
thousands: at Changping (260 BC) the Zhao army destroyed is given as
**~450,000** (400,000 prisoners executed plus battle losses), and Wang Jian's
army for the conquest of Chu (224 BC) as **600,000**. These are the famous
inflation cases.

**The inflation debate.** Michael Loewe and Edward Shaughnessy (eds., *The
Cambridge History of Ancient China*, 1999) regard such numbers as exaggerated,
on three grounds: inadequate record-keeping, figures far larger than
comparable pre-modern societies could field, and systematic incentives to
inflate — Qin soldiers were rewarded by enemy heads taken, and Han-era
historians had an interest in painting the pre-unification age as maximally
bloody (debate as summarized with attribution at [Warring States period —
Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Warring_States_period); the
underlying chapter is Mark Edward Lewis, ["Warring States Political
History"](https://www.cambridge.org/core/books/cambridge-history-of-ancient-china/1274DEAA9CE3A6D0AC700076B70D5C22),
in Loewe & Shaughnessy). A common counter-position in the same debate is that
a blanket divide-by-ten is too aggressive: armies of low hundreds of thousands
*cumulatively* (field force plus transport/logistics conscripts) near
navigable rivers are not physically absurd. A defensible scholarly reading:
**real fighting strengths plausibly ~⅓ to ~1× the recorded figures at the
extreme low/high ends; the recorded number often bundles combat troops with
conscripted porters and boatmen.**

**Structural mobilization.** What is NOT disputed is the institutional
machinery: Shang Yang-style household registration and universal male military
service (roughly ages 15/17 to 60 in Qin practice), the county (*junxian*)
system, and rank-for-merit — the state apparatus existed precisely to convert
registered population into armies and logistics columns (Lewis, *Sanctioned
Violence in Early China* and the Cambridge chapter above; institutional
summary at [Military of the Warring States —
Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Military_of_the_Warring_States)).

**Fractions.** If Qin proper held ~4–5M people and Wang Jian's 600,000 is
taken literally, ~12–15% of the whole population was in one field force — at
or beyond the plausibility ceiling for an agrarian state, which is the core of
the skepticism. Deflated to ~200–300k combat troops, the Chu campaign is
~5–7% of Qin's population under arms at peak — extreme but comparable to
other total-mobilization agrarian cases (cf. Shu Han §4). Cumulative
mobilization across the ten years of unification wars (230–221 BC) plainly
exceeded any single peak several times over — Qin fought sequential campaigns
against six states with recurring levies — but no reliable cumulative number
survives; *Shiji* casualty aggregates (repeated claims of 100,000+ beheaded
per campaign) are the most inflated part of the record and should not be
summed at face value.

**Anchor extracted:** peak field commitment claims of 10–15% of population are
recorded but scholarly-deflated to ~5–7%; institutionally, the *entire*
adult-male cohort (~25% of population) was register-liable.

## 2. Sengoku Japan — kokudaka/gun'yaku (the economy→quota case)

This is the cleanest historical case of "economy determines military quota,"
because the quota was written as an explicit linear formula on assessed
economic output.

**The system.** Hideyoshi's cadastral surveys (*taikō kenchi*) assessed every
domain in *koku* of rice yield (1 koku ≈ one person's annual rice consumption).
The *kokudaka* figure was explicitly the base "on which to calculate military
and other obligations due from the daimyō" ([Kokudaka —
SamuraiWiki](https://samurai-archives.com/wiki/Kokudaka); [Gunyaku — Japanese
Wiki Corpus](https://www.japanesewiki.com/history/Gunyaku.html)). *Gun'yaku*
(軍役) was the military-service obligation itself — men, horses, and provisions
owed per unit of assessed yield.

**Campaign quota values (Korea invasion mobilization orders, 1592).** The
mobilization decree scaled the levy by distance from the theater:
**Kyūshū lords (the spearhead) owed 5 men per 100 koku** (= 500 men per
10,000 koku), with domains farther from the front assessed at lower ratios
([The Imjin War — Nanban.pt](https://nanban.pt/articles/hideyoshi-invasion-of-korea/),
drawing on Kenneth Swope, *A Dragon's Head and a Serpent's Tail* (2009) and
Stephen Turnbull, *Samurai Invasion* (2002)). Japanese-language scholarship
(Fujiki Hisashi and the *Cambridge History of Japan* vol. 4 tradition) reports
the full schedule with slight variation — commonly rendered as **Kyūshū 5–6 per
100 koku, Chūgoku/Shikoku ~5, Kinai ~4, tapering to ~3 or fewer per 100 koku
further east**; treat the exact regional table as source-variant, but the
headline "≈500–600 men per 10,000 koku at the maximum campaign rate" is
robust. Sengoku-daimyo-internal levies ran in the same band or lower — one
reported internal rate is 5 men per 200 koku, i.e. 2.5 per 100
([survey with rates at Ashigaru — Grokipedia](https://grokipedia.com/page/Ashigaru);
low-confidence tertiary source, direction consistent with the rest).

**Peacetime/steady-state comparison.** The later Tokugawa service schedules
(e.g., the 1649 *gun'yaku* regulation) set a 10,000-koku obligation at roughly
**~235 men** — i.e. the *sustained* obligation was about **200–250 men per
10,000 koku**, roughly half the maximum campaign levy (regulation figures as
reported in the Cambridge History of Japan vol. 4 tradition and
[Kokudaka — SamuraiWiki](https://samurai-archives.com/wiki/Kokudaka); exact
schedule varies by rank and year). This gives a clean historical
**campaign-max : sustained-quota ratio of ≈ 2 : 1** on the same economic base.

**Actual mobilizations.**
- Kyūshū campaign (1587): Hideyoshi's coalition ~200,000–280,000 (Turnbull's
  figures as reported in campaign summaries; [Toyotomi Hideyoshi —
  Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toyotomi_Hideyoshi)).
- Odawara (1590): ~210,000 besieging
  ([Siege of Odawara (1590) — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siege_of_Odawara_(1590))).
- Korea, first wave (1592): **158,800** in nine divisions, **~183,800** with
  the Tsushima/Iki reserve divisions; **~320,000 total Japanese troops
  committed across the whole 1592–98 war** (figures per Turnbull/Swope/Hawley
  as compiled at [Japanese invasions of Korea —
  Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Japanese_invasions_of_Korea_(1592%E2%80%931598));
  a nationwide mobilization total of ~335,000 for the invasion is given at
  [Gunyaku — Japanese Wiki Corpus](https://www.japanesewiki.com/history/Gunyaku.html)).

**Fractions.** Japan's population in 1600 was **15–17 million** (William Wayne
Farris, [*Japan's Medieval Population*](https://uhpress.hawaii.edu/title/japans-medieval-population-famine-fertility-and-warfare-in-a-transformative-age/),
2006). So:
- Peak single mobilization (~200–210k): **~1.2–1.4% of population** actually
  under arms at once.
- Cumulative Imjin commitment (~320–335k over 6 years): **~2% of population**.
- Theoretical maximum levy: Japan's total assessed base was ~18.5 million koku
  (Nihon-koku kokudaka aggregates as reported at
  [Kokudaka — SamuraiWiki](https://samurai-archives.com/wiki/Kokudaka));
  at the spearhead rate of 5–6/100 koku that is ~0.9–1.1 million men, i.e.
  **~5–6% of population** — matching the "Sekigahara-era ~5–6% mobilizable"
  claim as a *ceiling of the quota system*, not an achieved deployment. The
  Sekigahara campaign itself put ~160–170k men on one field
  ([Battle of Sekigahara — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Sekigahara))
  with total campaign mobilization on all fronts plausibly around double that.

**Ratio for our dial (Imjin window):** total committed (~320–335k) over peak
standing deployment (~158–184k) ≈ **1.8–2.0× over a six-year war**.

**Anchor extracted:** the economy→quota law was linear and explicit:
**~500–600 men per 10,000 koku maximum campaign levy; ~200–250 per 10,000
sustained** — where 10,000 koku ≈ the food of ~10,000 people. Max levy ≈ 5–6%
of the fed population; sustained ≈ 2–2.5%.

## 3. Napoleonic France 1793–1815 (money remained, bodies ran out)

**Revolutionary base.** The levée en masse (decree of 23 August 1793)
declared all unmarried men 18–25 requisitioned; it brought army paper strength
to ~1.1–1.5 million by September 1794, of which **~800,000 effective** — the
paper rolls counted the sick, captured, and deserted ([Levée en masse —
Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lev%C3%A9e_en_masse); effective-vs-
paper analysis at [Organization of French Revolutionary Armies —
frenchempire.net](https://www.frenchempire.net/articles/armies/)). France's
population was ~28–30 million.

**Napoleonic conscription totals.** Between 1800 and 1813 the regime drafted
**more than 2.4 million** Frenchmen into the army or National Guard
(Louis Rousseau de la Combe-adjacent economic-history quantification:
["Drafting the Great Army: The Political Economy of Conscription in Napoleonic
France," *Journal of Economic
History*](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/journal-of-economic-history/article/drafting-the-great-army-the-political-economy-of-conscription-in-napoleonic-france/FDBA5D70BC85C24186EF7C9767D249BF)).
Totals near **2.5 million drafted, of whom ~1 million never returned** are the
standard summary ([Conscription in France —
Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conscription_in_France)). A French
analysis puts 1800–1814 conscription at ~2 million = **36% of all mobilizable
men and ~7% of the entire French population**
([Cairn / Napoleonica](https://www.cairn.info/revue-napoleonica-la-revue-2008-1-page-136.htm)).
Counting the revolutionary levies of 1791–94 (~0.75–1M more), the full
1791–1815 window drafted **~3–3.5M of ~29M ≈ 10–12% of population**.

**Peak standing force.** Effective French strength peaked around **~800,000**
(1794) and again around 600,000–1,000,000 under arms across all theaters at
the Empire's height; the 1812 Russia invasion force was ~600–685k, roughly
half of it French ([French Imperial Army —
Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Imperial_Army_(1804%E2%80%931815))).

**Exhaustion (the design-relevant beat).** After Russia destroyed the 1812
army, the state's fiscal machinery still functioned but the register was
spent: the senatus-consultum of 9 October 1813 called the classes of 1814 and
1815 *early* — 200,000 boys, minimum age effectively dropping toward 14–16 and
minimum height to 5'1" — the "**Marie-Louises**" who fought the 1814 campaign
undertrained ([Marie-Louise (conscript) —
Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marie-Louise_(conscript));
[napoleon.org, "Napoleon in 1813"](https://www.napoleon.org/en/history-of-the-two-empires/articles/napoleon-in-1813/)).
Napoleon demanded far more men in 1813–14 than France delivered; draft
evasion and exhausted year-classes, not bankruptcy, ended the war-making. This
is the canonical "**economy intact, register empty**" case.

**Ratio for our dial:** window-total conscripted (~2.4–3.5M) over peak
effective standing force (~0.8–1.0M) ≈ **~3–4× across the 15–22-year window**
(≈ 2.5–3× if only 1800–1815 is counted against the imperial peak).

## 4. Three Kingdoms China — register collapse and tuntian

**The census cliff.** The late Han census (permanent registers, 156/157 AD)
recorded ~10.7M households / **~56.5M people**; the Western Jin census of
280 AD after reunification recorded 2.46M households / **~16.2M people**
(administrative figures as carried in the standard histories, summarized at
[Three Kingdoms — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three_Kingdoms)).
Face value: a fall of ~70%.

**Scholarly view: mostly administrative, partly demographic.** The consensus
position (Rafe de Crespigny's third-century work is the standard
English-language authority; the argument is summarized in the survey
literature, e.g. [Three Kingdoms —
Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three_Kingdoms) and the discussion at
[Oreate, "Population Decline During the Three
Kingdoms"](https://www.oreateai.com/blog/historical-investigation-and-analysis-of-the-population-decline-during-the-three-kingdoms-period/76f6c07487edafeae7abeebd85372c77))
is that the registered figure measures **the state's ability to count and tax
people, not the people**: great-family estates absorbed commoners as
unregistered dependents (tax shelter), refugees moved off the rolls, and
soldiers plus military-colony households were registered on separate military
rosters outside the civil census. Real demographic loss from war, famine, and
epidemic was severe — but tens of millions did not literally die; plausible
actual third-century population is reconstructed well above the registered
16M (mid-20s to ~40M in various reconstructions). Treat "5/6 of China died"
as register artifact plus real loss, in unknown but roughly comparable
proportions.

**Military fraction of the registered base.** The surrender/conquest
enumerations preserved in the standard histories are famous data points: Shu
Han at its fall (263 AD) registered **940,000 civilians and 102,000 soldiers**
(~10.9% of the registered population under arms; figure as carried at
[Military history of the Three Kingdoms —
Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Military_history_of_the_Three_Kingdoms));
Wu at its fall (280) is traditionally given as ~2.3M registered with ~230,000
soldiers (~10%). Because the civil register undercounts, the *true* fraction
under arms was lower — plausibly **5–7% of actual population** — but the
states genuinely ran at total-mobilization intensity for decades.

**Tuntian — the economy–military coupling invention.** To feed armies from a
wrecked agrarian base, Cao Cao (from 196 AD, following proposals of Zao Zhi
and Han Hao) institutionalized *tuntian*: state-run military-agricultural
colonies of displaced people, refugees, and surrendered soldiers, farming
state land for a crop share earmarked to army supply, registered separately
from civil households ([Military history of the Three Kingdoms —
Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Military_history_of_the_Three_Kingdoms)).
Design translation: when the civil economy collapses, the state *creates* a
dedicated economy whose entire output is military quota — the extreme end of
economy-determines-quota, where the quota becomes the economy.

**Anchor extracted:** a war-exhausted agrarian state sustained ~10% of its
*registered* (≈5–7% of actual) population as standing soldiers for decades —
but only by fusing army and farm, and at the cost of the civil register
itself.

## 5. World War I — France and Germany (the total-war ceiling)

Administrative records here are firm; this is the empirical ceiling case.

- **France**: population ~39.6–40M; peacetime army (Aug 1914) **827,000**
  with 1,325,000 trained reserve; total mobilized over the war **8.4M ≈ 21–22%
  of total population** (≈44% of all males)
  ([Library of Congress, "Mobilized Strength and Casualty
  Losses"](https://www.loc.gov/collections/world-war-i-rotogravures/articles-and-essays/events-and-statistics/mobilized-strength-and-casualty-losses/);
  peacetime figures at [HistoryNet, "How Many Men Were
  Available"](https://www.historynet.com/how-many-men-were-available-for-military-service-in-germany-and-france/)).
- **Germany**: population ~67–68M; peacetime regulars **761,000** plus
  1,180,000 reserves; total who served 1914–18 **13.2–13.7M ≈ 19–20% of
  population** (13.67M served per the German medical-service statistics as
  reported at [1914-1918-Online, "War Losses
  (Germany)"](https://encyclopedia.1914-1918-online.net/article/war-losses-germany/);
  the Library of Congress compilation's 11M counts field mobilization more
  narrowly).

**Ratios for our dial:**
- Total-mobilized : *peacetime* standing army — France 8.4M / 0.83M ≈ **10×**;
  Germany 13.4M / 0.76M ≈ **18×**. (This is the wrong denominator for our
  game, where the ceiling is already a wartime ceiling, but it shows how small
  peacetime establishments were.)
- Total-mobilized : *peak wartime* strength — both armies peaked around
  4–5M (France) and 7–8M (Germany) under arms at once, giving
  **≈ 1.8–2.0× over a 4.3-year window** — the same turnover constant as the
  Imjin War.

## 6. Thirty Years' War 1618–1648 (the long-grind outlier)

- **Population base**: the Holy Roman Empire held roughly 18–21M people in
  1618. Modern estimates put total demographic loss at **15–20% of the
  Empire's population** (Peter H. Wilson, *Europe's Tragedy / The Thirty Years
  War*, 2009, is the standard modern treatment; range as summarized with
  attribution at [Thirty Years' War —
  Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thirty_Years%27_War)), against
  older textbook claims of 25–40% ([EBSCO Research
  Starters](https://www.ebsco.com/research-starters/military-history-and-science/thirty-years-war));
  regionally 50%+ (Mecklenburg, Pomerania, Württemberg).
- **Armies vs base**: total combatants deployed *inside Germany* averaged
  80,000–100,000 (1618–26), **peaked ~250,000 in 1632**, fell under 160,000 by
  1648 ([Thirty Years' War —
  Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thirty_Years%27_War)). Peak
  in-theater strength was thus only **~1.2–1.4% of the contributing
  population** — small standing fractions, because armies were
  contractor-recruited (Wallenstein raised ~50,000 for Ferdinand II; Gustavus
  Adolphus landed with ~18–20,000 and expanded with German recruits).
- **Turnover**: Wilson puts battle dead/wounded at ~450,000, with disease
  multiplying military deaths by 2–3× to **~1.3–1.8M military dead** — i.e.
  the armies *died* 5–7 times their peak strength over 30 years, implying
  total-who-served on the order of **7–10× peak strength across the whole
  window** (mercenary churn, re-enlistment, and forced recruitment of
  prisoners inflate this beyond any citizen-conscription case).

**Anchor extracted:** long low-intensity grinds have small peak fractions but
extreme cumulative turnover; this is the upper bound on register:peak, and it
is *above* what a compressed deciding-war window should model.

## Synthesis

### (a) Total mobilized over a decisive window, as fraction of population

| Case | Window | Fraction of total population |
|---|---|---|
| Sengoku Japan, Imjin commitment (actual) | 6 yr | ~2% |
| Sengoku Japan, quota-system ceiling (5–6/100 koku nationwide) | — | ~5–6% |
| Shu Han / Wu standing forces (registered base) | decades | ~10% registered ≈ 5–7% actual |
| Warring States peak claims, scholarly-deflated | campaign | ~5–7% (literal claims 12–15%, rejected) |
| Napoleonic France 1791–1815 cumulative draft | 22 yr | ~10–12% (7% for 1800–14 alone) |
| Thirty Years' War military dead alone | 30 yr | ~7–9% of population died as soldiers |
| WWI France / Germany total served | 4.3 yr | ~20–22% (industrial total war; ceiling) |

Pre-industrial agrarian states in existential wars cluster at **~5–12% of
total population passing through arms across the window**; ~20% is reachable
only with railways, tinned food, and mass bureaucracy. Peak *simultaneous*
strength for agrarian states clusters at 1–2% (comfortable) to 5–7%
(total-mobilization, economy-deforming — Shu Han needed tuntian to hold it).

### (b) Window-total-mobilized : peak standing force (the dial)

| Case | Window length | Ratio |
|---|---|---|
| Imjin War, Japanese side | 6 yr | **1.8–2.0** |
| WWI France | 4.3 yr | **~1.9** |
| WWI Germany | 4.3 yr | **~1.8** |
| Napoleonic France (1800–15 vs imperial peak) | 15 yr | **~2.5–3** |
| Napoleonic France (1791–1815 vs effective peak) | 22 yr | **~3–4** |
| Thirty Years' War (mercenary churn) | 30 yr | **~7–10** (outlier regime) |

The striking regularity: **intense wars of ~4–7 years refill the peak army
roughly twice; deciding-war eras of ~15–25 years refill it roughly 3–4
times.** Beyond that, the recruitment regime itself changes (mercenary churn,
child classes, register collapse). The Marie-Louises and the Three Kingdoms
register are what "past the right edge of the bracket" looks like — states
that kept paying but ran out of counted bodies.

### (c) The kokudaka economy→quota anchor

- **Maximum campaign levy: ~500–600 men per 10,000 koku** (5–6 per 100 koku,
  Kyūshū spearhead rate, 1592), tapering with distance from theater.
- **Sustained obligation: ~200–250 men per 10,000 koku** (Tokugawa gun'yaku
  schedules), i.e. **max-levy ≈ 2× sustained quota on the same economic
  base**.
- Since 10,000 koku ≈ annual food of ~10,000 people, the quota law reads:
  *sustained army ≈ 2–2.5% of the population your economy feeds; maximum
  short-window levy ≈ 5–6%.* This maps directly onto a "600 men per abstract
  population point" ceiling: the historical grammar says the *lifetime
  register* should stand to that ceiling roughly as max-levy-years stand to
  sustained-years — a small integer multiple, not an order of magnitude.

### Recommendation — register:cap ratio for a compressed deciding-war match

The game window (15–25 turns compressing a hegemonic contest) sits between
the WWI/Imjin regime (short, intense, ratio ~2) and the Napoleonic regime
(generation-long deciding era, ratio ~3–4). The Thirty Years' War regime
(7–10×) models mercenary churn across three decades and is out of scope; the
sub-2 regime makes the register nearly indistinguishable from the ceiling and
erases the "blood as permanent currency" identity.

**Recommended bracket: register:cap ≈ [2.5 – 4.0], point recommendation
3.0** (total lifetime draftable bodies ≈ 3× the sustained standing-force
ceiling; at 600 men/pop-point, a register of ~1,800 lifetime bodies per
point).

Properties of 3.0 against the anchors:
- A realm that loses its entire peak army twice can still rebuild once more —
  the Napoleonic arc (rebuild after 1812, break in 1814) is reproducible
  in-match.
- Cumulative-bodies sanity check: if one population point represents on the
  order of 20–30k people, a 1,800-body register is ~6–9% of that population —
  inside the 5–12% pre-industrial decisive-window envelope from (a).
- The last ~0.5× of the register is historically the degraded cohort
  (Marie-Louises); if the game later wants a quality penalty on the register's
  tail, the same sources anchor it.
- Below 2.5, the second war of a match already scrapes the register floor
  (WWI-in-4-years feel, too fast for a 15–25-turn arc); above 4.0, bodies
  stop being scarcer than money, contradicting the exhaustion identity the
  register exists to create.

### Source quality note

WWI figures are administrative-record grade. Napoleonic totals are
archival-scholarship grade (JEH; Forrest's conscription studies are the
standard monographs). Sengoku quota rates are documentary (mobilization
orders) but the exact regional schedule varies across secondary reports —
confidence in "≈500–600/10,000 koku spearhead max, ≈200–250 sustained" is
high, in any finer breakdown moderate. Three Kingdoms registered figures are
primary-administrative but measure state reach, not demography. Warring
States figures are contested at the factor-of-several level and are used here
only after explicit deflation.
