# Crisis → conquest-relief: historical plausibility of two mechanisms

**Evidence-layer document — 2026-07-12.** Per this repo's documentation law
(`.claude/rules/documentation-law.md`, Working/Record layer): research is an
**input to design seals, never normative on its own**. Nothing here is a
ruling; it is the historical basis a designer weighs before sealing a mechanic.

**Extends** the 2026-07-11 survey
(`research/2026-07-11-crisis-endgame-historical-precedents.md`), which already
established the *conquer-through-chaos vs consolidate-and-outlast* dichotomy,
the case table, the Cao Cao / Qingzhou seed, the Roman overextension paradox,
and the Thirty Years' War / Westphalia negotiated-draw counter-example. This
document does **not** re-litigate "is crisis→total-war plausible" (answered
there). It goes deeper on two specific proposed mechanics and asks whether they
are historically repeatable or one-off contrivances.

## The question

In pre-modern multipolar war eras (Warring States 戰國, Later Han / Three
Kingdoms 三國, Sengoku 戦国; comparatively the Late Roman Republic, the Russian
Time of Troubles, the 17th-century "general crisis"), when several states fell
into *simultaneous* internal crisis near an era's end, do these two mechanisms
have real, repeatable basis?

- **(a) Conquest relieves the conqueror's OWN internal crisis** — via plunder
  funding a strained center; conquered land/spoils buying off restive elites
  and paying mutinous soldiers; exporting malcontents into armies of conquest
  (pressure-release); rally-round-the-flag / diversionary war; eliminating the
  external sponsor or safe-haven of one's own rebels.
- **(b) Absorbing / co-opting the ENEMY's rebels, defectors, and population** to
  grow stronger and topple them.

Design context (history is the job; game is the reason it matters): a
fictional East-Asia-inspired turn-strategy game whose endgame is a "Match
cataclysm" — c. turns 25–35 all surviving states fall into internal rebellion
at once. The intent is that the cataclysm *drives* decisive inter-state
conquest, not universal turtling. The current build makes conquest *import* the
enemy's rebellion as a pure suppression burden (a Roman-paradox effect) while
map-wide rebellion blocks anyone from winning — producing a ~24% draw rate. The
team is weighing a fix where conquest *relieves* crisis and/or *absorbs* the
enemy's rebels as one's own strength. The designer's worry, in spirit: "is
'conquest relieves crisis' a gamey contrivance, or historically real?"

---

## Verdicts up front

**Mechanism (a) — conquest relieves the conqueror's own crisis:**
**GROUNDED ONLY UNDER SPECIFIC CONDITIONS.** Each sub-mechanism has genuine
historical instances *and* a matching failure case. The decisive variable is
**where the yield lands** — spoils/manpower captured *by the central state*
relieve; spoils/manpower captured *by field commanders* deepen the crisis
(the Roman paradox). "Export malcontents outward" and "rally-round-the-flag"
are real as *motives* but unreliable as *outcomes*: the diversionary-war
mechanism is one of the weakest-supported propositions in the empirical IR
literature.

**Mechanism (b) — absorbing the enemy's rebels/defectors:**
**HISTORICALLY GROUNDED** — this is the best-attested of the two, a *repeatable,
near-standard practice* across exactly the East Asian eras the game draws on
(Cao Cao 192, Guandu 200, Chu–Han 205–202 BCE, Qin's unification snowball, and
above all the Qing conquest of 1644, where defected manpower literally
outnumbered the conquerors). **Rider:** absorbed manpower is a strength *during*
the external war but a *deferred internal-order cost* — it tends to be loyal to
a person or to remain armed, so it disperses on a leader's death or turns on the
state once the common enemy is gone. Absorption is not a free win; it is a
loan against future stability.

---

## Mechanism (a): conquest relieving the conqueror's own crisis

Probed sub-mechanism by sub-mechanism, because the verdict differs across them.

### (a1) Plunder / spoils funding a strained center — CONDITIONAL

Real when the loot reaches the *treasury*; counter-productive when it reaches
the *general*.

- **Positive: Sengoku land redistribution.** Oda Nobunaga and Toyotomi
  Hideyoshi financed loyalty directly out of conquest: captured manors and
  temple estates (荘園) were re-granted to samurai and wealthy farmers, and
  power seats were placed at nodes of regional trade so revenue concentrated
  under central control (Berry, *Hideyoshi*, Harvard UP 1982). Here the yield
  routed *to the unifying center*, which is why it bought cohesion rather than
  fragmenting it.
- **Negative: the Late Roman Republic.** Pompey's eastern settlement (66–62
  BCE) and Caesar's Gallic plunder (58–50 BCE) were enormous — but the wealth
  and the land-for-veterans obligation attached to the *commander*, not the
  *res publica*. Spoils that stick to a field commander build a private army
  and a private clientela; that is the engine of the civil wars, not a relief
  of them (Syme, *The Roman Revolution*, Oxford UP 1939). Same act — capturing
  spoils — with the opposite systemic sign, decided purely by *who banks it*.

Net: plunder-relieves-center is grounded, but the condition (spoils flow to the
state, not the field commander) is the whole story.

### (a2) Conquered land buying off restive elites / paying mutinous troops — CONDITIONAL

Same conditional, one step further: it works, and it plants the next crisis.

- Sengoku enfeoffment (above) is the clean positive: new estates purchased the
  compliance of the samurai class and newly-subdued *daimyō*.
- **Double-edged: Qing enfeoffment of defectors.** After 1644 the Qing bought
  off its most valuable Ming turncoat, Wu Sangui, with hereditary rule over
  Yunnan/Guizhou and a ~100,000-man private army. It secured the conquest — and
  directly set up the **Revolt of the Three Feudatories (1673–1681)**, when Wu
  turned that granted power against the throne (Wakeman, *The Great
  Enterprise*, California UP 1985). Buying off elites with conquered land
  *relieves now, bills later*.

### (a3) Exporting malcontents by conscription — REAL AS MOTIVE, UNRELIABLE AS OUTCOME

The single cleanest East Asian analogue is the **Imjin War (1592–1598)**.

- The "pressure-valve" reading is explicit in the scholarship: with unification
  complete, Japan held on the order of 100+ *daimyō* and a very large veteran
  warrior class *with no wars left to fight*; a foreign campaign gave that
  energy an outward outlet and tested the loyalty of recently-subdued lords
  (discussed in Berry, *Hideyoshi*; Turnbull, *Samurai Invasion*, 2002). This is
  a genuine, contemporary-recognized instance of "conscript the restless and
  point them abroad."
- **But it is contested and it *failed*.** Kenneth Swope (*A Dragon's Head and
  a Serpent's Tail*, Oklahoma UP 2009) cautions against reducing Hideyoshi's
  aims to a safety valve — grandiose conquest-of-Ming ambition was real. More
  important for the game: the *outcome* was strain, not relief. The war
  stalemated, drained the Toyotomi treasury and its most committed vassals'
  strength, and is widely read as a contributor to the Toyotomi collapse at
  Sekigahara (1600) and Osaka (1615). The pressure valve, once opened, bled the
  regime that opened it.

Verdict on (a3): grounded as a *motive a ruler can plausibly hold*; **not**
grounded as a reliable *relief* — historically it as often accelerated the
exporter's fall.

### (a4) Rally-round-the-flag / diversionary war — WEAKLY SUPPORTED (name the limits honestly)

This is the sub-mechanism the design should lean on *least*, because it is
precisely where the empirical record is thinnest.

- **Jack S. Levy, "The Diversionary Theory of War: A Critique"** (in *Handbook
  of War Studies*, ed. Midlarsky, Unwin Hyman 1989): the founding critical
  review found a persistent gap between rich qualitative/historical narratives
  of diversion and a weak, inconsistent quantitative record — the theory is
  intuitive but under-confirmed.
- **M. Taylor Fravel, "The Limits of Diversion"** (*Security Studies* 19:2,
  2010): the two most-cited modern "easy cases" for diversion — Argentina's
  1982 Falklands seizure and Turkey's 1974 Cyprus invasion — on close reading
  give *surprisingly little* support; security-interest explanations fit at
  least as well. Fravel's broader territorial-dispute work finds leaders facing
  serious internal unrest have frequently *compromised* externally (to buy
  quiet on one front) rather than escalated — the *opposite* of diversion.

So "internal crisis automatically makes a state lash outward" is not a law you
can bank on. If the game wants outward aggression under crisis, it should be
driven by the concrete resource/manpower mechanics of (a1)/(a2)/(b), not by a
morale/diversion abstraction the historical record only weakly supports.

### (a5) Eliminating the external sponsor / safe-haven of one's own rebels — THIN

Logically sound, historically real as a *contributing rationale* rather than a
documented prime mover of a conquest. Qin's unification propaganda framed
neighbors as harborers of threats; the Time of Troubles shows the *inverse*
(Poland sheltered the False Dmitrys, giving Muscovy every reason to want that
haven gone — see counter-evidence). Treat this as a flavor rationale a mechanic
can *cite*, not a load-bearing empirical pillar.

## Mechanism (b): absorbing / co-opting the enemy's rebels and defectors

This is where the history is strong, specific, and repeatable — a standard
operating procedure of exactly the eras the game evokes.

### (b1) The Cao Cao / Qingzhou seed (192 CE), in depth

- **What happened.** In 192, remnant Yellow Turban bands from Qing Province
  (青州) — hundreds of thousands of people with families — poured into Yan
  Province. After the incumbent inspector Liu Dai was killed, Cao Cao took over,
  defeated the bands, and negotiated a *mass surrender*: sources give ~300,000
  fighters (with a much larger following of dependents) submitting.
- **How the manpower was integrated.** Cao Cao skimmed the best into an elite
  standing unit, the **Qingzhou troops (青州兵)**, ~30,000 strong, and settled
  the mass of surrendered population into **tuntian (屯田) military-agricultural
  colonies** — soldier-farmers on state land producing their own supply. This
  is the crucial move: the rebels were not merely *defeated* or *garrisoned*,
  they were *converted into a self-financing manpower-and-grain base* that
  solved Cao Cao's supply problem and fed the campaigns culminating at Guandu
  (200). (de Crespigny, *Imperial Warlord: A Biography of Cao Cao*, Brill 2010;
  *Fire over Luoyang*, Brill 2016.)
- **The double-edge, exactly on point for the synthesis.** The Qingzhou troops
  were bound to *Cao Cao personally*, not to the institution. At his death in
  220, they **"beat their drums and dispersed"** on their own; the minister Jia
  Kui, rather than treat it as mutiny, issued them travel passes and provisions
  to disperse home peacefully (Sanguozhi, Jia Kui biography; recounted in de
  Crespigny). Absorbed rebel manpower was a decisive *asset while the man who
  absorbed it lived and led it* — and evaporated the moment that personal bond
  broke.

### (b2) Was it repeatable in the same period? Yes — repeatedly.

- **Battle of Guandu (200 CE) — defection cascade.** Cao Cao beat the far
  larger Yuan Shao largely *through* the enemy's own disaffected: the advisor
  Xu You defected and revealed the Wuchao supply depot; after its destruction,
  the front-line generals Zhang He and Gao Lan — scapegoated and fearing
  execution — surrendered their forward units, collapsing Yuan Shao's line.
  Enemy defection was not a footnote; it was the mechanism of victory.
- **Chu–Han Contention (206–202 BCE) — stealing the rival's talent and troops.**
  Liu Bang assembled his winning coalition largely from men who had defected
  from Xiang Yu's Chu — Han Xin, Ying Bu (King of Jiujiang), Peng Yue — bought
  with promises of land and kingship, and they converged to destroy Xiang Yu at
  Gaixia (202 BCE) (Lewis, *The Early Chinese Empires: Qin and Han*, Harvard UP
  2007). **Double-edge again:** once the external enemy was gone, that same
  absorbed strength read as an internal threat — Liu Bang and Empress Lü purged
  Han Xin, Peng Yue, and Ying Bu (executed 196 BCE). Absorbed power loyal to
  *itself* becomes the next crisis.
- **Qin's unification snowball (230–221 BCE).** Qin's method was explicitly
  cumulative: conquered "lands, labor, soldiers, and revenue were folded into
  the machinery that destroyed the next state," while spies and bribery (the Li
  Si / Wei Liao program) kept the other six from forming a grand coalition —
  each faced Qin increasingly *alone* (Lewis 2007). This is (b) as a *conveyor
  belt*: each conquest materially finances and mans the next.
- **The strongest, most systematic case — the Qing conquest of China (1644 →).**
  The Manchu conquest was carried out *primarily by absorbed enemy manpower*.
  Han Chinese who defected before 1644 were enrolled as **Han bannermen**; the
  inflow was so large that ethnic Manchus were **only ~16% of the Eight Banners
  by 1648, with Han bannermen ~75%.** Wu Sangui's defection opened Shanhai Pass
  (May 1644); surrendered Ming troops after 1644 were folded into the Green
  Standard Army, which came to outnumber the Banners roughly three to one, and
  defected Han troops routinely served as the *vanguard* of conquest (Elliott,
  *The Manchu Way*, Stanford UP 2001; Wakeman, *The Great Enterprise*,
  California UP 1985). A small crisis-born power conquered a vastly larger one
  chiefly by making the enemy's defectors and rebels into its own army. This is
  mechanism (b) at full institutional scale — and it is *not* an outlier; it is
  the culmination of a pattern visible from Chu–Han onward.

### (b3) Backing pretenders / fifth columns — GROUNDED

The active-subversion form of (b): rather than wait for defectors, manufacture
them. Qin's bribery-and-spies coalition-splitting (above) is the East Asian
case. The comparative case is the Time of Troubles, where the Polish szlachta
*sponsored* successive False Dmitry pretenders against the Muscovite crown —
though note that there the fifth column served the *stronger* neighbor
preying on a collapsing state, which is the counter-pattern below.

## Counter-evidence (weighed honestly — the more common pattern may be the opposite)

The design's hope is that crisis → outward conquest. The historical *base rate*
tilts the other way: simultaneous internal crisis more often makes a state
**prey**, and conquest itself more often **worsens** internal order. Three
strands:

### C1. Prey, not predator — weakness invites partition

- **Russian Time of Troubles (1598–1613).** Dynastic extinction, famine
  (1601–03), and civil war did *not* turn Muscovy outward; they turned it into
  a carcass that non-crisis neighbors carved up. Poland-Lithuania and Sweden
  intervened, backed pretenders, and by 1610–12 Poles held the Kremlin and
  Swedes held Novgorod — "a failed state" partitioned from outside (Truce of
  Deulino 1618). The internally-broken state was the *object*, not the agent, of
  conquest.
- **The general crisis of the 17th century.** Geoffrey Parker (*Global Crisis:
  War, Climate Change and Catastrophe in the Seventeenth Century*, Yale UP 2013;
  and the earlier *General Crisis of the Seventeenth Century* debate) documents
  that in the 1640s a wave of *simultaneous* internal crises produced
  synchronized **state breakdown**, not synchronized outward conquest: Ming
  China collapsed, Poland-Lithuania disintegrated, the Spanish Monarchy shed
  Portugal and Catalonia, the Stuart monarchy fell into civil war, Russia and
  France (the Fronde) convulsed. When *everyone* is in crisis at once, the modal
  outcome is a field of collapsing states, not a triumphant conqueror. This is
  the sharpest single caution for the "all states rebel at once → decisive
  conquest" design premise.

### C2. Conquest worsens internal order — the Roman overextension paradox

Restated because it is the direct mechanical inverse of the design's wish:
bigger conquests → larger, longer-serving armies whose reward expectation
(land, plunder) binds them to their *general* rather than the state → those
armies become instruments of *domestic* conflict → the loser faces annihilation,
so compromise dies. Rome's greatest era of conquest *was* its era of internal
collapse; the two were causally linked, not coincidental (Syme, *The Roman
Revolution*, 1939). Inheriting ungovernable territory and general-loyal armies
is a *cost centre*, which is exactly the "import the enemy's rebellion as a
suppression burden" effect the current build already models.

### C3. Exhaustion → negotiated draw, not a single conqueror

From the prior survey and worth holding: the Thirty Years' War (1618–48) shows
that mounting devastation and fiscal/desertion collapse did **not** escalate to
an unlimited war with one winner — it raised the cost of decisive victory until
the parties settled at Westphalia with *multiple survivors* (Wilson, *Europe's
Tragedy*, Harvard UP 2009). Exhaustion can *produce* the negotiated stalemate
the design is trying to avoid. A cataclysm that only raises pain, without also
creating an exploitable *opportunity* (defectors to absorb, spoils to bank),
tends historically toward the draw, not the conquest.

### C4. Diversion is weakly supported (see a4)

The IR literature (Levy 1989; Fravel 2010) is the empirical brake on assuming
crisis *reliably* converts to outward aggression. Real, but far from automatic.

## Synthesis: the variables that separate relieving-conquest from straining-conquest

The history is consistent on *why* the same act (conquer during crisis)
strengthens Cao Cao and the Qing but shatters Rome and the Toyotomi. Six
variables, each stated so it maps onto a dial:

1. **Yield destination — center vs field commander.** Spoils and manpower that
   route to the *conquering state* relieve (Sengoku enfeoffment, Cao Cao's
   tuntian). Spoils that stick to the *field commander* fund warlordism and
   deepen the crisis (Pompey/Caesar). → *Mechanic:* who receives the conquest
   payout — the player's central treasury/levy pool, or a semi-autonomous
   general/province — should decide whether conquest reduces or raises internal
   disorder.

2. **Integration depth — absorbed-and-re-tasked vs merely occupied.** Enemy
   manpower *converted into a self-sustaining unit* (Qingzhou troops + tuntian;
   Han bannermen; Green Standard Army) is a resource. Territory *held as a
   garrison* over a cohesive hostile population is a policing liability (the
   Roman inheritance; the current build's suppression burden). → *Mechanic:*
   distinguish "absorb the enemy's rebels as new levy/supply" (relief) from
   "occupy the enemy's land" (cost). Only the former should relieve.

3. **Loyalty binding — institution vs person.** Manpower bound to the *state*
   is durable; manpower bound to a *person* disperses on his death (Qingzhou
   troops in 220) or, if left armed and enfeoffed, turns on the state once the
   common enemy is gone (Han Xin's purge 196 BCE; the Three Feudatories 1673).
   → *Mechanic:* absorbed enemy strength should carry a **deferred cost** —
   post-cataclysm revolt risk, or a bond that lapses on a leader/event — so
   absorption is a loan, not a free win.

4. **Administrative/logistic capacity to digest.** Relief requires the center
   can actually *pay, feed, and command* the new manpower. Cao Cao's tuntian
   made absorption self-financing; the Toyotomi supply line to Korea could not
   sustain the force it exported. → *Mechanic:* absorption should be gated by a
   supply/administration capacity; exceeding it flips absorption from asset to
   burden (over-extension).

5. **Speed vs the crisis clock.** Fast, decisive absorption *before one's own
   crisis matures* wins (Qin's snowball; Cao Cao pre-Guandu). Overextending
   *while already mid-rebellion* shatters both fronts (Imjin → Toyotomi fall;
   the late Republic). → *Mechanic:* conquest-relief should reward striking
   early in the cataclysm window and punish opening a new front when one's own
   disorder is already high.

6. **Availability of a genuine fifth column.** Absorption presupposes the
   enemy's people/troops are *actually disaffected and available* (surrendering
   Yellow Turbans, scapegoated Zhang He, defecting Wu Sangui). Against a
   *cohesive* population there is nothing to absorb — only territory to occupy,
   which strains. → *Mechanic:* the amount of relief a conquest yields should
   scale with the *target's own rebellion/disorder level* — you harvest the
   enemy's crisis, you do not harvest a stable enemy.

**One-line design read:** conquest during the cataclysm should *relieve* the
attacker only when it (i) routes spoils/manpower to the central state, (ii)
*converts the defeated enemy's own rebels* into paying/self-sustaining strength
rather than occupying hostile land, and (iii) is done fast and within
administrative capacity — and it should still carry a *deferred* internal-order
bill (person-loyal or still-armed absorbed troops). That is historically
faithful, it directly attacks the turtling/draw problem by making a rival's
collapse an *exploitable opportunity* rather than only a shared pain, and it
keeps the Roman-paradox failure mode alive as the punishment for doing it
wrong.

## Sources

Named academic historians / university-press works and the empirical IR
literature are preferred; reference works are used only for uncontested
chronology and are flagged as such.

**Chinese antiquity — Warring States, Qin, Han, Three Kingdoms**
- Mark Edward Lewis, *The Early Chinese Empires: Qin and Han* (Harvard University Press, 2007) — Qin unification snowball; Chu–Han defections and post-war purges.
- Rafe de Crespigny, *Imperial Warlord: A Biography of Cao Cao 155–220 AD* (Brill, 2010) — Qingzhou troops (青州兵), tuntian colonies, Guandu, the 220 dispersal.
- Rafe de Crespigny, *Fire over Luoyang: A History of the Later Han Dynasty 23–220 AD* (Brill, 2016) — Yellow Turban absorption and the provincialization of military power.
- Primary source underlying the Cao Cao/Jia Kui material: *Sanguozhi* (Chen Shou), Jia Kui biography — the Qingzhou troops "beating drums and dispersing" at Cao Cao's death.

**Sengoku Japan and the Imjin War**
- Mary Elizabeth Berry, *Hideyoshi* (Harvard University Press, 1982) — land redistribution to buy elite loyalty; the idle-warrior "outlet" reading of the Korea invasion.
- Kenneth M. Swope, *A Dragon's Head and a Serpent's Tail: Ming China and the First Great East Asian War, 1592–1598* (University of Oklahoma Press, 2009) — cautions against the reductive "safety-valve" motive; the war as strain, not relief.
- Stephen Turnbull, *Samurai Invasion: Japan's Korean War 1592–1598* (Cassell, 2002) — narrative and the pressure-valve interpretation (popular-scholarly).

**Ming–Qing transition**
- Frederic Wakeman Jr., *The Great Enterprise: The Manchu Reconstruction of Imperial Order in Seventeenth-Century China* (University of California Press, 1985) — Ming defector absorption; Wu Sangui; the Three Feudatories double-edge.
- Mark C. Elliott, *The Manchu Way: The Eight Banners and Ethnic Identity in Late Imperial China* (Stanford University Press, 2001) — Han bannermen; Manchus as a minority within the Banners.

**Late Roman Republic**
- Ronald Syme, *The Roman Revolution* (Oxford University Press, 1939) — spoils/veterans bound to generals; the overextension → civil-war paradox. (Elite-politics slant noted in the prior survey.)

**Russian Time of Troubles / 17th-century general crisis**
- Geoffrey Parker, *Global Crisis: War, Climate Change and Catastrophe in the Seventeenth Century* (Yale University Press, 2013) — synchronized internal crisis → state breakdown, not synchronized conquest.
- Geoffrey Parker & Lesley M. Smith (eds.), *The General Crisis of the Seventeenth Century* (2nd ed., Routledge, 1997) — the comparative crisis debate.

**Thirty Years' War**
- Peter H. Wilson, *Europe's Tragedy: A History of the Thirty Years War* (Harvard University Press / Penguin, 2009) — exhaustion → negotiated multi-survivor settlement.

**Diversionary war / rally-round-the-flag (empirical IR)**
- Jack S. Levy, "The Diversionary Theory of War: A Critique," in Manus I. Midlarsky (ed.), *Handbook of War Studies* (Unwin Hyman, 1989) — theory/history rich, quantitative record weak.
- M. Taylor Fravel, "The Limits of Diversion: Rethinking Internal and External Conflict," *Security Studies* 19, no. 2 (2010): 307–341 — Falklands/Cyprus "easy cases" give little support; unrest can push leaders toward external *compromise*.
