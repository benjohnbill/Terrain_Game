# Garrison:Field Ratio and Armed-Peace Standing Levels — Historical Survey

Status: RESEARCH (evidence layer — input to seals, never normative on its own)
Date: 2026-07-07
Purpose: Ground two match-arc dials in historical scholarship:
(1) the garrison:field ratio at war footing — what fraction of a mature
state's total soldiery is static/territorial (fortress, border, city
garrisons) versus mobile field forces; (2) the "armed peace" level — in a
pre-war standoff between mature rival powers with fortresses standing, how
full field armies run relative to their war-footing maximum.

Game context (short): compressed multipolar deciding-war era
(Warring-States-like). Each realm's military = mobile FIELD ARMY (capped at
1/3 of conscription register — sealed) + static GARRISONS (border-fortress
shields + capital guard, locally capped).

Method note: where numbers are contested the range is given; paper strength
(establishment on the rolls) is distinguished from effective strength
(soldiers actually present and usable) wherever the scholarship makes the
distinction.

---

## 1. Late Roman Empire — limitanei vs comitatenses

The clearest premodern case of an explicit static/mobile split: *limitanei*
(border/frontier troops under duces, tied to their sectors) versus
*comitatenses* (mobile field armies, including *palatini* and praesental
escort armies).

**Total establishment (contested):**

- A.H.M. Jones (1964): ~600,000 empire-wide excluding fleets — but this is
  widely read as official (paper) rather than actual strength
  ([Late Roman army — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Late_Roman_army)).
- Warren Treadgold (1995): ~581,000 under Constantine I, ~514,500 at the
  Notitia Dignitatum date; East in 395 ~300,000–352,000 following Jones
  ([Late Roman army — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Late_Roman_army);
  [Eastern Roman army — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eastern_Roman_army);
  Treadgold, *Byzantium and Its Army, 284–1081*, Stanford UP 1995).
- Hugh Elton: ~410,000 under Constantine; "low count" revisionists put the
  effective army at ~350,000–400,000, near 2nd-century levels
  ([Late Roman army — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Late_Roman_army)).

**Field share of the total:**

- Constantinian period: the expanded comitatus held roughly **a quarter of
  total regular forces** (~25% of ~400,000)
  ([Late Roman army — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Late_Roman_army)).
- c. 395 East: Treadgold counts the five eastern comitatus armies at
  ~20,000 each, ~100,000–104,000 total — **one-third to one-half of the
  eastern army** depending on which total is accepted
  ([Eastern Roman army — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eastern_Roman_army);
  [Palatini — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palatini_(Roman_military))).
- The three praesental escort armies alone (~75,000) were ~20–25% of total
  effectives ([Late Roman army — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Late_Roman_army)).

**Paper-vs-effective flag (load-bearing for the ratio):** R. Duncan-Jones's
reassessment concluded Jones overestimated limitanei unit sizes by **2–6
times**; papyri and archaeology suggest frontier legions averaged ~500 men,
not Jones's ~3,000
([Late Roman army — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Late_Roman_army);
[Limitanei — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Limitanei)).
Consequence: on paper the static share is ~65–75% (field 25–35%); on
effective strength the field share plausibly rises toward **40–50%**,
because the inflation is concentrated in the static half. Both readings are
live in the scholarship.

**Takeaway:** paper split static:field ≈ 2:1 to 3:1; effective split
plausibly ≈ 1:1 to 1.5:1. Note the late Roman posture is *defensive
imperial perimeter*, not a multipolar deciding war — its static share is a
ceiling, not a model.

## 2. Warring States China

No direct scholarly percentage for static vs field distribution survives;
the evidence is structural plus rhetorical numbers from the *Zhanguo ce* /
*Shiji* persuader speeches.

- States walled their frontiers and erected border garrisons; Qin's King
  Zhaoxiang wall carried a permanent watchtower-and-garrison network
  ([watchtower archaeology, Shaanxi](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC12380348/);
  [Warring States period — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Warring_States_period)).
- Universal male conscription with rotation: under Shang Yang's system Qin
  males were called at 16 for a year of local training plus a year at the
  capital, with only one male per family liable at a time to protect
  agriculture ([Military of the Warring States — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Military_of_the_Warring_States)).
  Adult males additionally owed frontier garrison service (*shu* 戍) — the
  garrison establishment was thus a standing rotation drawn from the same
  register as field conscripts (Lewis, *Sanctioned Violence in Early
  China*, SUNY 1990, ch. 2 — the standard treatment of the mass-mobilization
  state).
- Rhetorical order-of-magnitude: the persuader inventory for Wei counts
  ~100,000 garrison troops and porters against a ~300,000-man total army —
  a **~1/3 static-plus-logistics share** at full mobilization
  (*Zhanguo ce* figures, discussed at
  [Historum: Manpower and size of armies in Warring States](https://historum.com/t/manpower-and-size-of-armies-in-warring-states.191284/)).
  These are advocacy numbers; scholars treat them as mobilization
  *potentials*, not standing establishments.
- Qin at the unification wars: modern conservative assessments put Qin's
  permanently maintained forces at ~200,000–500,000 within a claimed
  ~1,000,000 total including garrisons, conscripts, and militias
  ([Qin Military overview, Dragon's Armory](http://dragonsarmory.blogspot.com/2026/01/qin-army-warring-states-era-overview.html)).
  Immediately post-unification Qin posted ~300,000 under Meng Tian on the
  northern frontier — a static-frontier commitment on the same order as a
  major field army (*Shiji* 88, standard account; see
  [Warring States period — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Warring_States_period)).

**Takeaway:** in the deciding-war phase the field army dominates —
static+logistics ≈ 1/4–1/3 of a fully mobilized host — but a permanent
professional core (picked troops like Wei's *wuzu*, capital guards, wall
garrisons) stands between campaigns while the conscript mass returns to the
land ([Military of the Warring States — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Military_of_the_Warring_States)).

## 3. Sengoku Japan

Quantitative scholarship on the home-garrison share is thin; the evidence
is case-based (numbers below are from campaign records, not establishment
tables).

- Mobilization was assessed per vassal income in koku (*gun'yaku*): each
  vassal supplied a set number and type of troops per unit of assessed
  yield ([Gunbai: Sengoku Period Warfare Part 1](http://gunbai-militaryhistory.blogspot.com/2018/03/sengoku-period-warfare-part-1-army-and.html)).
  There was no separate garrison establishment: the same register fed both
  the marching army and the castle keepers (*rusui*) left behind.
- Sekigahara 1600 (offensive posture): Ieyasu marched ~70,000 on the
  Tōkaidō while Hidetada took ~30,000 on the Nakasendō; he deliberately
  left only ~2,000 under Torii Mototada to hold Fushimi — a sacrificial
  tripwire, not a shield
  ([Battle of Sekigahara — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Sekigahara);
  [Siege of Fushimi Castle — Grokipedia](https://grokipedia.com/page/Siege_of_Fushimi_Castle)).
  On the Western side, Ishida's home castle Sawayama retained only ~2,800
  after the field army departed
  ([Battle of Sekigahara — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Sekigahara)).
  Reading: when a decisive field battle was sought, **on the order of
  90%+ of mobilized strength marched**; castles were stripped to skeleton
  keepers.
- Odawara 1590 (defensive posture, the inverse): the Hōjō pulled ~50,000
  into Odawara castle and distributed the rest across their branch-castle
  network against ~200,000+ Toyotomi besiegers — essentially **the whole
  mobilization went static**
  ([Siege of Odawara 1590, Sengoku Period Wiki](https://sengoku-period.fandom.com/wiki/Siege_of_Odawara_Castle_(1590))).
  The Hōjō *shukuro*/branch-castle system is the canonical
  fortress-network realm; its failure mode (each castle reduced in detail
  while the field army never formed) is itself a design-relevant datum.

**Takeaway:** the static/field split in Sengoku is *posture-dependent*, not
a fixed establishment ratio — near-zero static share on the decisive
offensive, near-total static share in a fortress defense. Between
campaigns, only the retinue core (samurai households, retained ashigaru in
the castle towns) stood ready; the levy mass was re-raised per campaign
([Gunbai, ibid.](http://gunbai-militaryhistory.blogspot.com/2018/03/sengoku-period-warfare-part-1-army-and.html)).

## 4. Ming China — weisuo and the Nine Garrisons

- Paper establishment: the early-Ming *weisuo* (guard-battalion) system
  carried **over 2.7 million troops on the rolls**, organized in *wei* of
  5,600 ([Military of the Ming Dynasty, Baidu wiki](https://baike.baidu.com/en/item/Military%20of%20the%20Ming%20Dynasty/1491375);
  [chinaknowledge: Ming military](http://www.chinaknowledge.de/History/Ming/ming-military.html)).
  This establishment was overwhelmingly territorial/static and notoriously
  hollow: self-sustaining farm-soldier units decayed, and "during military
  campaigns, only the best fighters among the units were sent out to war"
  ([chinaknowledge, ibid.](http://www.chinaknowledge.de/History/Ming/ming-military.html)).
- The Nine Garrisons (九邊) of the northern border/Great Wall held
  **~300,000 soldiers and officers** across a network later expanded to
  eleven garrisons, with 1,100-odd fortified settlements and thousands of
  posts and beacon towers
  ([Nine Garrisons of the Ming dynasty — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nine_Garrisons_of_the_Ming_dynasty);
  [MPIWG: Military Defense System of the Great Wall](https://www.mpiwg-berlin.mpg.de/research/projects/military-defense-system-great-wall-ming-dynasty)).
- The mobile counterweight was the capital's Three Great Training Divisions
  (Five Armies, Three Thousand, Firearms) — picked men skimmed from the
  static establishment
  ([chinaknowledge, ibid.](http://www.chinaknowledge.de/History/Ming/ming-military.html)).

**Takeaway:** on paper the Ming ran a static share of well over 80%; in
effective terms the usable army *was* largely the border garrisons plus the
capital divisions — a defensive-perimeter empire like late Rome, and like
late Rome an upper bound rather than a model for a deciding-war era.

## 5. Early-modern Europe — fortress garrisons vs field armies

The strongest quantified anchors, because states kept establishment tables.

- **Dutch Republic, 1629:** the States Army counted 77,000 soldiers, from
  which Frederick Henry could raise a **mobile field army of 28,000**, the
  rest fixed in garrisons — field share **~36%, garrison ~64%**
  ([Eighty Years' War 1621–1648 — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eighty_Years%27_War,_1621%E2%80%931648)).
- **Spanish Army of Flanders, 1639:** just under half of the 77,000-man
  army was distributed across **208 small garrisons** — garrison share
  **~50%** ([Army of Flanders — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Army_of_Flanders);
  the underlying count is G. Parker, *The Army of Flanders and the Spanish
  Road*, Cambridge UP 1972).
- **Vauban-era France:** Vauban himself estimated that in **1678, 1694 and
  1705, between 40 and 45% of the French army was assigned to garrison
  duty** ([Vauban — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/S%C3%A9bastien_Le_Prestre,_Marquis_of_Vauban);
  context in J.A. Lynn, *Giant of the Grand Siècle: The French Army,
  1610–1715*, Cambridge UP 1997).

**Takeaway:** in the fortress-saturated multipolar wars of 1600–1715 —
the closest structural analog to a fortress-anchored deciding era with
professional standing armies — the war-footing static share converges on
**40–65%**, i.e. garrison:field between roughly **0.7:1 and 1.8:1**, with
the offensive great power (France) at the low end and the small
fortress-belt defender (Dutch) at the high end.

## 6. Armed-peace standing levels

How full did field forces run in a standoff, relative to war maximum?

**Reserve-system era (1914) — the LOW anchor, wrong analog:**

- France, spring 1914: peacetime strength **882,907**; August 1914
  mobilization put **3,780,000** men under arms — standing fraction
  **~23% of immediate war strength**
  ([French Army in World War I — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Army_in_World_War_I);
  [1914 French mobilization — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1914_French_mobilization)).
- Germany held a comparable ratio (peacetime ~0.8M vs ~4.5M at the
  outbreak) ([Britannica: WWI forces and resources 1914](https://www.britannica.com/event/World-War-I/Forces-and-resources-of-the-combatant-nations-in-1914);
  [History Crunch: Germany in WWI](https://www.historycrunch.com/germany-in-world-war-i.html)).
- The ~1:4–1:5 standing:mobilized ratio depends entirely on the trained
  conscript-reserve system; the great powers "could only afford to keep a
  fraction of these men in uniform in peacetime"
  ([1914-1918-Online: Arms Race prior to 1914](https://encyclopedia.1914-1918-online.net/article/arms-race-prior-to-1914-armament-policy/)).

**Pre-reserve-system eras — the relevant band:**

- **France under Louis XIV:** peacetime establishments stabilized around
  **130,000–140,000** after major peaces (post-Nijmegen, post-Ryswick),
  against real wartime maxima of **~350,000–400,000** (Nine Years' War
  peak just over 350,000 effective) — standing fraction **~35–40% of war
  footing** ([Grokipedia: French Royal Army](https://grokipedia.com/page/French_Royal_Army),
  figures per Lynn, *Giant of the Grand Siècle*).
- **Dutch Republic:** wartime 62,000 (1607) and 70,000 (1635 peak);
  post-1648 peacetime 30,000 (1650), fluctuating 40,000–53,000 through the
  armed-peace decades 1667–1680 — standing fraction **~45–75% of war
  footing** depending on threat level, i.e. a standoff-sensitive dial the
  Dutch actively turned
  ([Dutch States Army — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dutch_States_Army)).
- **Warring States between campaigns:** standing component = professional
  picked corps, capital guard, and rotating wall/frontier garrisons; the
  conscript mass demobilized to the fields, re-raised per campaign under
  universal registration (one male per family liable at a time)
  ([Military of the Warring States — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Military_of_the_Warring_States);
  Lewis, *Sanctioned Violence in Early China*). No scholarly percentage
  exists; structurally this resembles a standing core of well under half
  of campaign maximum, with fast re-mobilization because registers and
  training rotation already existed.
- **Sengoku between campaigns:** the retinue core (samurai households,
  retained ashigaru concentrated in castle towns) stood permanently; levy
  mass was campaign-raised via gun'yaku assessment
  ([Gunbai, ibid.](http://gunbai-militaryhistory.blogspot.com/2018/03/sengoku-period-warfare-part-1-army-and.html)).
  Not quantified in accessible scholarship — flagged as evidence-thin.

**Takeaway:** in pre-reserve, standing-professional eras the armed-peace
standing level runs **~35–60% of war-footing maximum** (Louis XIV low-mid
band, Dutch mid-high band under direct threat), versus ~20–25% only after
the conscript-reserve revolution. Garrisons, by contrast, stayed
near-permanently manned in peace across all eras surveyed (limitanei,
Nine Garrisons, Dutch fortress belt) — the peace draw-down falls on the
field army, not the fortress shield.

## 7. Synthesis table

| Era | Static share of total (war footing) | Field share | Armed-peace standing, as fraction of war max | Evidence quality |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Late Rome (paper, Jones/Treadgold) | 65–75% | 25–35% | n/a (standing professional, no peace draw-down) | Good, contested totals |
| Late Rome (effective, post-Duncan-Jones) | ~50–60% | ~40–50% | n/a | Inferential |
| Warring States (full mobilization) | ~25–35% incl. logistics | ~65–75% | standing core well under 1/2 of campaign max; fast re-raise | Rhetorical numbers, structural scholarship |
| Sengoku offensive (Sekigahara-type) | ~5–10% | ~90%+ | retinue core only; unquantified | Case-based, thin |
| Sengoku defensive (Odawara-type) | ~90–100% | ~0–10% | — | Case-based |
| Ming (paper) | >80% | <20% | garrisons permanently manned | Good on paper, hollow in fact |
| Dutch Republic 1629 | ~64% | ~36% | ~45–75% (1650–1680: 30–53k vs 62–70k war) | Strong |
| Army of Flanders 1639 | ~50% | ~50% | — | Strong (Parker) |
| Vauban's France 1678/94/1705 | 40–45% | 55–60% | ~35–40% (Lynn: 130–140k peace vs 350–400k war) | Strong |
| Pre-1914 Europe | (fortress garrisons minor) | — | ~20–25% (conscript-reserve system) | Strong, wrong analog |

Closest analogs to a fortress-anchored multipolar deciding era with
standing professional/retinue armies and **no** conscript-reserve system:
**early-modern Europe 1600–1715** (fortress-saturated, multipolar,
war-deciding field armies) for the ratio, cross-checked against **Warring
States / Sengoku offensive posture** (field-dominant when the decision is
sought). Late Rome and Ming are defensive-perimeter empires — upper bounds
on static share, not models.

## 8. Recommendations

**(a) Garrison:field ratio at war footing, ρ = garrison total ÷ field-army
max: [0.6 – 1.0], suggested pin 0.75.**
Derivation: the closest-analog band (Vauban 40–45% static, Flanders ~50%,
Dutch 64%) converts via ρ = g/(1−g) to 0.67 / 1.0 / 1.78. The Dutch value
reflects a small state hiding behind a fortress belt; a realm expected to
seek decision should sit at the France/Flanders end. Warring-States and
Sengoku offensive evidence (static ≤ 1/3) argues against pushing ρ above
1.0 for a deciding era; late-Rome/Ming values (1.9–3.0 paper) belong to
defensive perimeter empires and should be reserved, if at all, for a
hermit/turtle posture, not the baseline.

**(b) Armed-peace field-army fill, as fraction of field max standing in a
pre-war standoff: [0.4 – 0.6], suggested pin 0.5.**
Derivation: pre-reserve standing fractions — Louis XIV ~0.35–0.40, Dutch
armed-peace decades ~0.45–0.75 under direct threat, Warring
States/Sengoku standing cores structurally below 0.5 with fast re-raise.
The 1914 value (~0.2–0.25) requires a trained-reserve institution the
setting does not have and should be treated as a hard floor, not an
analog. Note the cross-era regularity worth preserving in the model:
peace draw-down falls on the field army while garrisons stay near fully
manned — the fortress shield is the constant, the field army is the
variable.
